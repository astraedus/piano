## Philosophy Notes — How the Piano Pedagogy Maps to Guitar

Before the content, here is how the existing app philosophy applies to guitar:

**Five-pillar structure holds exactly.** Technique / Repertoire / Ear / Expression / Lead-sheet (for guitar: chord chart / tab reading) maps cleanly. Keep all five alive from Phase 1.

**Chain logic is the same.** Every technique must chain: Skill → Chord/Riff → Progression → Improv/Song. A power chord is not a power chord. It is the engine of "Iron Man," "Enter Sandman," and every Green Day song. Show the chain immediately.

**Guitar equivalent of Chang's primitives:**
- Segment isolation first (one riff fragment clean before full riff)
- Slow + burst method for speed (same neuromotor logic — slow build, short fast bursts)
- Practice backwards (last bar of a riff first, then join)
- Relaxation as central skill (fretting-hand death-grip is the #1 beginner error on guitar; relaxed thumb, curved fingers)

**ADHD accommodation holds.** Short drills (2-4 min), reward songs early, visible progress, no shame on miss.

**One guitar-specific addition:** "One-minute changes" (JustinGuitar's core drill — count how many clean chord swaps you can do in 60 seconds). This is the guitar analog of Chang's parallel-set bursts — it builds automaticity through micro-repetition at target tempo. Every chord node gets this drill.

## Part 1 — Technique & Concept List

Ordered beginner → intermediate. Each entry follows the format: **Short Name | What it is | Why it matters | Concrete practice drill.**

---

### TIER 0 — Setup & Orientation (pre-technique, Day 1)

**T0-A: Guitar Anatomy & Tuning**
What it is: Names of strings (EADGBE low to high), frets, nut, bridge, headstock, tuning pegs. Using a chromatic tuner or phone tuner app.
Why it matters: You cannot communicate with anyone about your guitar — teacher, tab, app — without shared vocabulary. Tuning is non-negotiable; 10 minutes of out-of-tune practice reinforces wrong pitches neurologically.
Drill: Name every open string aloud, then tune each to a chromatic tuner. Do this at the start of every session until automatic (target: under 90 seconds).

**T0-B: Holding the Guitar & Right-Hand Pick Grip**
What it is: Seated classical vs. casual position. Pick held between thumb and index, tip exposed ~4mm. Relaxed wrist, forearm on guitar body.
Why it matters: Poor posture creates tension patterns that become injury ceilings exactly as they do at piano. This is the guitar equivalent of Alexander technique's "primary control."
Drill: 30-second body check: neck free, pick-hand wrist relaxed, fret-hand thumb behind the neck (not hooked). Do this before every practice block.

**T0-C: Reading Tab Basics**
What it is: Six lines = six strings (bottom line = thickest string E). Numbers = fret positions. Vertical stack = chord. Horizontal sequence = melody/riff. Bar lines divide measures. Time signature (4/4 most common).
Why it matters: 99% of guitar learning material uses tab. This is the guitar's "lead sheet reading" — the practical notation that unlocks the entire internet of resources.
Drill: Read and play back "Seven Nation Army" tab by number identification alone (don't play yet — just say the fret numbers aloud). Confirm every bar line.

### TIER 1 — Beginner Foundations (Phases 1-2 equivalent)

**T1-A: Fretting Hand Placement — Curved Fingers, Relaxed Thumb**
What it is: Press strings close to the fret (not on it, not far behind), with fingertip not pad, thumb behind the neck at roughly the middle joint of the index finger. No death-grip.
Why it matters: Every advanced technique — bends, barre chords, vibrato — depends on the hand architecture established here. Bad placement creates buzzing, muted strings, and RSI. This is the single highest-leverage mechanical correction for a beginner.
Drill: Press the 1st fret of every string individually. Each string must ring clearly with no buzz. Slowly walk up the fretboard to the 5th fret and back. One pass = one drill rep. 2 minutes.

**T1-B: Down-Picking (Single Downstroke)**
What it is: Pick moves downward (toward the floor) across strings. The most fundamental picking motion.
Why it matters: Before strumming, before alternate picking — the basic attack must be clean, consistent, and relaxed. Over-gripping the pick is the most common error.
Drill: Single downstrokes on the open E string (6th string) with a metronome at 60bpm. Quarter notes only. Wrist loose, pick angle ~45°. 1 minute. Then move to one-minute on open A, open D.

**T1-C: Alternate Picking**
What it is: Alternating down and up strokes (↓↑↓↑) strictly. The most efficient picking motion for single-note playing.
Why it matters: Alternate picking is required for any scale run or melodic line above moderate tempo. Learning it late means unlearning bad habits. JustinGuitar introduces this in Grade 1.
Drill: Chromatic exercise: fret 1-2-3-4 on the E string with alternate picking at 60bpm. Move to the A string, D string, G, B, e. One pass through all 6 strings = one rep. 3 minutes.

**T1-D: Open Chords — Em, Am, E, A**
What it is: The four most fundamental open chords. Em (two fingers), Am (three fingers, barre later), E major (three fingers), A major (three fingers across one fret). All use open strings as part of the chord.
Why it matters: These four shapes cover an enormous range of rock and pop songs in their natural keys. They also directly prepare the two main barre chord shapes (E-shape barre and A-shape barre). This is the chord foundation from which everything extends. JustinGuitar makes these central to Grade 1.
Drill: "One-Minute Changes" between Em and Am: count how many complete, clean changes you make in 60 seconds. Target 30+ changes before moving on. Repeat for E→A. This is the JustinGuitar core drill and the guitar equivalent of Chang's parallel-set bursts for chord automaticity.

**T1-E: Open Chords — D, G, C**
What it is: D major (three fingers in a triangle), G major (three or four fingers — 2nd and 3rd string open), C major (three-finger arc).
Why it matters: Together with T1-D these seven chords (Em, Am, E, A, D, G, C) cover the vast majority of pop and rock repertoire in common keys. G and C are particularly important for the G major key context.
Drill: One-Minute Changes: G→C (one of the hardest transitions for beginners). Record your count each session. The count going up is the progress metric. Add D→G once G→C reaches 30/minute.

**T1-F: Basic Strumming — Down-Only, Then D-DU, Then D-DU-UDU**
What it is: Strumming patterns built progressively. Down-only quarter notes → down-down-up (eighth note start) → the "folk strum" pattern D-DU-UDU.
Why it matters: Rhythm is the first thing listeners hear. A clean chord played with good rhythm sounds musical immediately. The pattern progression from simple to complex means there is always a "good enough" sound while building skill.
Drill: Strum the chord progression Em→Am→C→G (1 chord per bar, 4/4) with down-only strumming first. Once clean, add D-DU. Once clean, add full folk strum. Each stage: 3 minutes.

**T1-G: Power Chords (5th Dyads)**
What it is: Two- or three-note shapes: root + 5th (+ optional octave). Moveable — same shape slides anywhere on the fretboard. Played on the low strings (E, A, D). Written as "X5" (e.g. E5, A5, G5).
Why it matters: Power chords are the defining rhythm guitar sound in rock, punk, metal, and grunge. They are much easier than full chords (only 1-2 fret fingers needed), give instant "electric guitar sound" when distorted, and are the learner's first moveable shape — teaching fretboard geography. JustinGuitar covers power chords in Grade 2.
Drill: Play E5 (open E + 2nd fret A) and A5 (open A + 2nd fret D) alternating, 4 beats each, with a palm mute. Move E5 up to F5 (1st fret E + 3rd fret A). Move in half-steps up to 5th fret. 3 minutes. This is the "Iron Man" riff shape.

**T1-H: Palm Muting**
What it is: Lay the pick-hand's heel lightly on the strings immediately at the bridge saddle. Produces a chunky, percussive, shortened sustain. Lift slightly for open sound.
Why it matters: Palm muting transforms power chords from "loud" to "rhythmic." It is the defining texture of punk, metal, and rock rhythm guitar. Without it, power chord strumming sounds homogeneous. The on/off control of muting is the guitar's equivalent of staccato vs. legato.
Drill: Alternate 2 bars palm-muted E5 / 2 bars open E5, with downstrokes at 80bpm. The goal is a clean, audible contrast between muted and open on every bar line switch. 2 minutes.

### TIER 2 — Early Intermediate (Phases 2-3 equivalent)

**T2-A: Hammer-Ons**
What it is: Pick a fretted note, then sound a higher note on the same string by forcefully "hammering" a finger onto the fret without picking again. Written in tab as "h" (e.g. 5h7).
Why it matters: Hammer-ons create legato flow between notes — the guitar equivalent of slurring on piano. They are the first building block of lead guitar phrasing. Every pentatonic lick uses them. Berklee's tab guide lists hammer-ons as one of the first notation symbols learners must read.
Drill: On the G string, finger fret 5, pick, then hammer fret 7 firmly. The hammered note must be equal volume to the picked note. 10 reps, then move the shape to every string. 2 minutes.

**T2-B: Pull-Offs**
What it is: Finger a higher fret, pick the note, then "pull" the fretting finger downward and off the string — the fingertip plucks the string in the process, sounding a lower fretted note. Written as "p" (e.g. 7p5).
Why it matters: Pull-offs are the downward complement to hammer-ons. Together they enable legato runs (the guitar's "slur" language) and are essential for the blues-rock phrasing in every riff and solo.
Drill: Fret G string at both 5 and 7 simultaneously. Pick fret 7, pull off to fret 5 cleanly. The pulled note must ring clearly. 10 reps per string. 2 minutes. Combine with T2-A: pick 5, hammer 7, pull 5 — a complete "hammer-pull" cell.

**T2-C: Slides**
What it is: Fret a note, pick it, then slide the same finger to a new fret while maintaining string pressure. Ascending slide (/) or descending slide (\). Written in tab as "/" or "sl" (e.g. 5/7).
Why it matters: Slides are the smoothest connection between notes — zero attack on the landing note, maximum melodic glide. They are heard in nearly every blues and rock solo and are much easier to execute than bends. They also teach fretboard distance in a physical, intuitive way.
Drill: On the B string, pick fret 5 and slide to fret 7. The slide should take approximately one beat at 80bpm. Repeat descending (7 to 5). Then try quick slides: fret 5 to 7 in a half beat. 2 minutes.

**T2-D: String Bending (Whole Step)**
What it is: Fret a note and push the string toward the ceiling (or pull toward the floor on high strings) to raise pitch. A whole-step bend raises pitch by two semitones (one whole tone). Written in tab as "b" (e.g. 7b9 = bend from 7 up to sound of 9).
Why it matters: Bending is the most expressive single technique in guitar. It mimics the human voice sliding to a note. A clean, in-tune whole-step bend on the B string (standard blues move) is an enormous milestone that unlocks the entire vocabulary of blues-rock lead playing. This is the guitar's equivalent of the "voice" in piano expression.
Drill: Fret the B string at the 7th fret with your ring finger (support with middle and index behind it). Bend upward until the note sounds like the 9th fret. Compare to the actual 9th fret note. Target: bend lands in tune within 2 cents. 5 reps, 2 minutes. Rest hand between sets — bending is physically demanding.

**T2-E: Vibrato**
What it is: Rapid, small oscillation of a bent note — the string is pushed slightly sharp and released repeatedly, creating a wavering pitch effect. Different from bending: vibrato stays at its target pitch while oscillating around it.
Why it matters: Vibrato is how a guitarist "sings" a held note. Without vibrato, a sustained note is dead. With vibrato, it breathes. This is the single biggest distinguisher between a mechanical player and one who sounds musical on lead guitar. It comes after bending because the mechanics are the same — controlled string deflection.
Drill: Bend the B string at fret 7 up a quarter-step, then oscillate down and back 4 times per beat at 60bpm. The oscillations must be even. Record yourself and listen back — good vibrato is instantly recognizable. 2 minutes.

**T2-F: Minor Pentatonic Scale — Box 1 (Root on 6th String)**
What it is: A 5-note scale (root, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭7) in a "box" pattern starting at the root on the 6th string. In A minor pentatonic, this is the 5th-position box (5th fret). The most used scale shape in all of rock.
Why it matters: The minor pentatonic is the beginner's soloing vocabulary. It cannot produce "wrong" notes over a minor blues or rock progression. Every classic rock solo — Clapton, Page, SRV, Slash — uses this as the primary vocabulary. JustinGuitar calls this "THE most important scale for a beginner guitarist." This is the guitar's "improvisation ladder I3" exactly.
Drill: Play Box 1 ascending and descending with alternate picking at 80bpm. Then: over a backing track in Am (freely available on YouTube for any tempo), improvise only within Box 1 for 2 minutes. No notes are wrong. Add slides and hammer-ons to make it musical.

**T2-G: Minor Pentatonic — Box 2 + Connecting Boxes**
What it is: The second of five pentatonic box positions, starting at the ♭3 of Box 1. Connecting Box 1 → Box 2 along the fretboard.
Why it matters: Knowing only one box traps the player in one neck position. Connecting boxes opens the full fretboard and enables the "travel" heard in longer solos. Guitar Orb and JustinGuitar both emphasize introducing Box 2 once Box 1 is fluent, not learning all five at once.
Drill: Play Box 1 up to the highest note, then continue into Box 2 without stopping. Descend back through both. 80bpm, alternate picking. 2 minutes. Then add one slide or bend per phrase to make it musical.

**T2-H: Barre Chords — E Shape**
What it is: The open E major chord shape moved up the neck, with the index finger barring all six strings at the capo fret. F major = E shape barred at fret 1. B major = E shape at fret 7. The shape is moveable to any root.
Why it matters: E-shape barre chords give access to any major chord anywhere on the neck. Together with A-shape barres (T2-I), the player can play in any key with full chords — no longer limited to the open-chord keys. This is a huge milestone and is genuinely hard. The CAGED system and JustinGuitar both flag this as the end of "beginner" territory.
Drill: F major barre chord (E shape, fret 1). Barre index firmly, then add fingers 2, 3, 4. All strings must ring. Hold for 4 counts, release completely, re-fret. 10 clean reps is the session target. "One-Minute Changes" between Bm and F#m (both E-shape barres, natural pair). Count goes up over weeks.

**T2-I: Barre Chords — A Shape**
What it is: The open A major chord shape moved up the neck, index finger barring frets 1-5 on strings 2-6 (not the 6th string). Bb major = A shape at fret 1. B major = A shape at fret 2. Common variation: "mini-barre" with index barring strings 2-4 only.
Why it matters: A-shape barres give access to any chord with the root on the A string, completing the "two moveable shapes" toolkit. Together with E-shape barres, these two shapes cover every chord in any key across the full neck.
Drill: B major barre chord (A shape, fret 2). One-Minute Changes between B major and E major (open). Target 20+ clean changes per minute.

**T2-J: Rhythm Reading in Tab — Counting Bars, Note Values**
What it is: Understanding that tab measures are divided by bar lines. Quarter note = 1 beat. Eighth note = half beat. Dotted rhythms. The "numbers under the tab staff" showing rhythm. Time signature understanding (4/4, 3/4).
Why it matters: Tab without rhythm notation is pitch-only — the learner guesses timing from memory of the song. Tab with rhythm notation is a complete score. This is the guitar's "sight-reading" skill. It unlocks learning from written material without needing to hear the song first.
Drill: Take a simple four-bar tab (e.g. the chorus of "Knockin' on Heaven's Door") and tap the rhythm with your foot before playing any notes. Verify each bar has the correct number of beats. Then play with a metronome at 60bpm.

### TIER 3 — Intermediate Rewards (Phases 3-4 equivalent)

**T3-A: 12-Bar Blues Structure**
What it is: The I-IV-V chord progression repeated over 12 bars, the foundation of blues, early rock and roll, and much of classic rock. In A: bars 1-4 = A (I), bars 5-6 = D (IV), bars 7-8 = A (I), bars 9 = E (V), bar 10 = D (IV), bars 11-12 = A (I) with a turnaround.
Why it matters: Understanding the 12-bar blues means understanding the harmonic skeleton of roughly half the classic rock and blues repertoire. It is also the primary context for applying pentatonic improvisation — the structure provides the harmonic home that makes the scale feel purposeful.
Drill: Play a slow 12-bar blues in A using power chords (E5, A5, B5) at 70bpm. Then repeat using open chords (E, A, E7, B7). Then solo over it with pentatonic Box 1. Three passes, 3 minutes each.

**T3-B: Lead Phrasing — Question and Answer**
What it is: Guitar soloing organized as two-bar "questions" and two-bar "answers." A phrase rises (tension), resolves (rest). The space between phrases is as important as the notes.
Why it matters: Phrasing is what makes a solo sound like music rather than scale exercises. This is the guitar analog of the piano's Expression ladder. Most beginners play too many notes continuously — teaching question/answer installs the "breathe" habit.
Drill: Over a 12-bar backing track, restrict to 4 notes per phrase with 1 bar of silence after. Then try: play one note, hold it with vibrato, wait, play one more. 2 minutes. This is borrowed from Chang's "expression from Day 1" approach.

**T3-C: Pentatonic Box 1 — Bends and Licks**
What it is: Learning 3-4 canonical blues-rock licks within Box 1: the "box 1 bend" (ring finger bends B string at fret 10 in Am), the "hammer-on lick" (7h8-5-7 on G string), the "call-response" (the SRV/Clapton move), the "pentatonic run" (all six strings ascending with alternate picking).
Why it matters: Licks are the vocabulary — "words" that the guitarist can deploy spontaneously. Transcribing and memorizing licks from real recordings is the guitar analog of the piano's "transcription is the ultimate ear drill." A learner with 5 memorized licks can improvise immediately. A learner with only scales cannot.
Drill: Learn one lick per week. Memorize it by ear from a recording (e.g., the "Sunshine of Your Love" intro as a lick). Transpose it to two other keys. This is the guitar equivalent of piano Improvisation Ladder I7.

**T3-D: Full-Neck Pentatonic Navigation**
What it is: Moving fluently between all five pentatonic box positions as one connected scale across the neck. Positional awareness: knowing where you are by fret number and box.
Why it matters: Locking into one box sounds amateur. Moving freely between boxes is what distinguishes "knows the pentatonic" from "plays lead guitar." This is an intermediate milestone that opens composition and longer solos.
Drill: "The Pentatonic Highway": start in Box 1 at the 5th fret, play to the top of Box 1, continue into Box 2, continue to Box 3, reach the 17th fret, descend back through all five boxes. Do this slowly (60bpm) as navigation practice, not speed. 3 minutes.

**T3-E: String Bending Accuracy + Expressive Bends**
What it is: Bending not just to "somewhere sharp" but to a specific target pitch (whole step, half step, step-and-a-half). Pre-bending (bend before picking, release to normal pitch). Over-bending as expression.
Why it matters: Inaccurate bends make a solo sound out of tune. Accurate, expressive bends are the most distinctive voice feature in blues and rock guitar. B.B. King, Stevie Ray Vaughan, and David Gilmour all built their signature sound primarily on bend accuracy.
Drill: Whole-step bend on B string fret 7. Play fret 9 first as the target. Then bend fret 7 until it matches fret 9. Repeat 10 times. Record and listen back for tuning accuracy. 2 minutes.

**T3-F: Rhythm Guitar — Syncopation and Accents**
What it is: Emphasizing off-beats in strumming/picking patterns (the "and" of beats). Classic rock rhythm patterns: the "ska strum" (muted downstrokes on beats, chord on upbeats), the "funk mute" (aggressive percussive muting), syncopated accents in riffs.
Why it matters: Rhythm accuracy is more important than technique speed for ensemble playing. Syncopation is what makes rhythm guitar feel alive rather than mechanical. This is the guitar equivalent of the piano's "rubato and rhythmic accent" expression skills.
Drill: Strum the open Em chord with this pattern: D-x-DU-x-DU (x = muted stroke, no chord). 4/4 at 80bpm. The muted strokes should be audible and percussive. 3 minutes.

## Part 3 — How to Show It Simply: Visualization Recommendations

### Per-concept visualization guide

**Chord diagrams (grid view)** — for any chord shape: Em, Am, power chords, barre chords. Grid = 6 vertical strings × 5 horizontal frets, with dots showing finger placement, X for muted strings, O for open strings. This is already universally understood. Implementation: a simple SVG grid per chord node, generated from a compact data format (strings: `[x, 0, 2, 2, 1, 0]` for Am).

**Fretboard map** — for scales (pentatonic boxes). A horizontal fretboard diagram (12-15 frets wide, 6 strings tall) with colored dots for scale tones. Root dots in a different color (e.g. orange), other scale tones in a lighter color. Show one box at a time; add a "see full neck" toggle that reveals all 5 boxes at once. This is more visually informative than standard chord boxes for scales.

**Tab with rhythm notation** — for riffs and melodies. The six-line tab staff with numbers and rhythm stems below. Bar lines clearly shown. Technique symbols inline (h, p, /, b, ~). This is the primary reading format for guitar content. The app should render tab as styled HTML/CSS (not images) so it scales to screen size.

**Animation / ghost hand** — for techniques that depend on motion (bends, vibrato, slides). A simple CSS/SVG animation showing the string deflecting during a bend is enormously more useful than a static diagram. Even a 2-second looping GIF showing the bend motion is worth more than a paragraph of text. Priority for animated treatment: bends, vibrato, hammer-ons (the "hammering" motion), palm muting (hand position at bridge).

**Audio reference** — every technique node should have a 2-5 second audio clip: the technique done correctly, then done incorrectly. The learner pattern-matches by ear. This mirrors the piano app's "ear moment" design.

**"Easy to view" for guitar specifically:**

- Never use standard music notation (treble clef) as the primary format for guitar. Guitarists read tab. Notation is an optional parallel display for learners who also read music.
- Chord diagram aspect ratio: portrait, 5 frets tall × 6 strings wide, finger numbers inside dots, barre shown as a curved bracket.
- Fretboard diagrams: landscape orientation, fret numbers labeled, nut shown at left for open-position shapes, an indicator of which fret position when up the neck.
- Tab: minimum font size 16px for fret numbers. Technique symbols should be distinguishable at a glance (color-coded: h = blue, b = orange, ~ = green, / = yellow, p = red) rather than relying on small text abbreviations alone.
- The "what does this sound like" button is a first-class UI element for every technique node — guitar learners orient by ear far more than piano learners because of the prevalence of tab over notation.

## Part 4 — Starter Repertoire

Ordered by difficulty, mapped to the skill tree nodes they unlock/require.

| # | Song | Artist | Techniques Required | Tree Nodes Needed | Why It Works |
|---|------|--------|--------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Seven Nation Army** | The White Stripes | Single-note riff, one string, downpicking | g-t1-downpick, g-t0-tab | 7 notes, one string, recognizable within 10 minutes. Perfect "I'm playing guitar now" moment. Teaches fret navigation without chord changes. |
| 2 | **Smoke on the Water** | Deep Purple | 4-note two-string riff, downpicking, power chord shape | g-t1-downpick, g-t1-power (intro), g-t0-tab | 4 notes per phrase, two adjacent strings, iconic beyond words. Introduces two-string coordination and the "parallel 4ths" interval shape that is the basis of many rock riffs. |
| 3 | **Iron Man** (riff) | Black Sabbath | Power chords, downstrokes, slow tempo | g-t1-power, g-t1-palmmute | Slow, heavy, uses the E5/G5/A5/D5 power chord shapes in one riff. Introduces power chord movement. Palm muting makes it menacing immediately. |
| 4 | **Sunshine of Your Love** (riff) | Cream | Minor pentatonic lick, hammer-on, open E string drone | g-t2-pent-box1, g-t2-hammer, g-t0-tab | The first "real" blues-rock riff. Uses Box 1 pentatonic with a hammer-on and ends on the open E drone. Teaches pentatonic vocabulary in a musical context. Clapton plays it slow and fat — expression immediately. |
| 5 | **Come As You Are** | Nirvana | E-shape power chord riff, open strings, moderate tempo | g-t1-power, g-t1-palmmute, g-t1-altpick | Introduces mixed open-string/fretted riff texture. Palm muting throughout. Nirvana riffs are pedagogically ideal: medium speed, clear rhythm, iconic. |
| 6 | **Back in Black** (intro riff) | AC/DC | Power chords, rhythm accents, percussive playing | g-t1-power, g-t1-palmmute, g-t3-syncopation | The quintessential rock rhythm riff. Alternating palm muted and open power chords, strong rhythmic accents. Introduces syncopation naturally. |
| 7 | **Whole Lotta Love** (riff) | Led Zeppelin | Minor pentatonic phrasing, string bending (slight), blues feel | g-t2-pent-box1, g-t2-bend, g-t3-phrasing | Page's riff is essentially pentatonic Box 1 phrasing with blues bends and question-answer structure. Learning this teaches phrasing, not just patterns. The riff IS the theory lesson. |
| 8 | **La Grange / Pride and Joy** (solo excerpt) | ZZ Top / SRV | Minor pentatonic Box 1 + Box 2, bends, vibrato, 12-bar context | g-t2-pent-box1, g-t2-pent-box2, g-t2-bend, g-t2-vibrato, g-t3-blues12 | First "real solo" milestone. Over a 12-bar blues context, using two pentatonic boxes, with bends and vibrato. This is the Tier 3 unlock reward — "I can improvise over blues." |

**Milestone notes:**

- Songs 1-2 are the "Day 1 reward" — learnable in a single session.
- Songs 3-4 are the "end of Week 2 reward" — power chords + first pentatonic lick.
- Songs 5-6 are the "Phase 1 complete" reward — rhythm guitar is functional.
- Songs 7-8 are the "Phase 2 complete" reward — lead guitar vocabulary is real.

The key principle from the piano app carries over: **every technique must chain into a real song in the same session.** "You just learned power chords and palm muting — here is the Iron Man riff, go." The song at the end is the point.

## Data-Model Note for the Builder

The guitar tree follows the same JSON schema as the piano curriculum, with these additions:

```jsonc
{
  "instrument": "guitar",
  "visualizationType": "chord_diagram" | "fretboard_map" | "tab" | "animation",
  "tabSymbols": ["h", "p", "/", "\\", "b", "~", "x"],
  "openStrings": ["E2", "A2", "D3", "G3", "B3", "E4"],
  "cagedShape": "E" | "A" | "G" | "C" | "D" | null
}
```

The `visualizationType` field drives what the UI renders per node — chord diagram SVG, fretboard grid, tab renderer, or animation embed. This is the main guitar-specific schema addition vs. the piano curriculum.
